What, then, is a cloud service? Cloud services can be rudimentarily defined as computer system resources that are accessed on demand without requiring the user‚Äôs direct and active management (DiMaria, 2020). Most notably, these services encompass data storage and computing power, which companies like Netflix, Pinterest, Instagram, etc. harness to resolve existing IT demands or to expand new services. Through cloud service providers, firms can simply pay for the IT infrastructure they need instead of navigating the upfront cost and complexity of developing their own infrastructure and data centers (DiMaria, 2020). Take Netflix, for example. The popular streaming service deals with large spikes in traffic during evenings, significantly subsiding in volume as the night progresses into a.m. hours. When Netflix‚Äôs viewership outpaced the capacity of its traditional data centers, it turned to a cloud service provider to manage its alternating periods of high demand and lulls in activity. More and more companies across a multitude of industries are turning towards cloud solutions and providers, a trend magnified by the increased reliance on virtual platforms due to the coronavirus pandemic. In fact, GlobalNewswire anticipates the global cloud computing market size to grow from 371.4 billion USD in 2020 to 832.1 billion USD by 2025 (2020).

If cloud service providers are on the rise, so too are the environmental consequences of their services. Within the interminable server halls of data centers, air conditioners are working constantly to quell the heat released by the engines processing digital requests, transactions, etc. Ironically, these air conditioning systems must burn carbon in order to dispel heat. In a 2019 report led by climate and computing engineer Maxime Efoui-Hess, digital technology‚Äîthe elusive, hazy presence we call the Cloud‚Äîwas found to have a greater carbon footprint than the air transport industry‚Äîthe noisy, hundreds-of-thousands-ton airplanes painting their exhaust trails in the sky. In the most efficient conditioning systems, water is cooled and then irrigated throughout server racks as a more effective convective agent than air, but this comes with a high price of millions of gallons of water daily (Monserrate, 2022). Moreover, cloud service providers do not intend for their infrastructure to minimize energetic cost. Similar to how Bitcoin cryptocurrency is inherently designed to reward computational power, and therefore energy consumption, data centers are ‚Äúa Russian doll of redundancies‚Äù: every system is supplemented with back-up systems in order to guarantee that users don‚Äôt face an interruption in their online experience (Monserrate, 2022). Even in death, cloud services take a toll on the environment. When the cables, batteries, transformers, etc. of servers are dismantled, they are sentenced to the toxic fate of most e-waste: overflowing landfills and often falling upon already-disadvantaged countries and communities to deal with.

Clearly, our regular usage of cloud services comes with a very material and real-world burden. The convenience and immediacy of the next episode queuing on Netflix, Amazon‚Äôs one-click purchasing option, saving files to Dropbox, and so forth can all be traced back to an environmental strain imposed by cloud service providers.
